Naomi,

Sharing customer-facing language you can use.

What we can say (approved):

A) “Do you cache DEKs? If KMS is down, can you still decrypt?”
- Redwood uses envelope encryption. Data is encrypted with a data encryption key (DEK), which is wrapped by a customer-managed key encryption key (KEK) in the customer’s KMS/HSM.
- For active workloads, Redwood may retain short-lived in-memory cryptographic material to serve requests efficiently (e.g., within a process lifetime). We do not rely on indefinite caching of customer KMS responses.
- If the customer KMS becomes unavailable, Redwood may be unable to unwrap DEKs for new decrypt operations that require a KMS call. In those cases, behavior is to fail fast and surface a clear error rather than risk silent corruption.
- We support operational controls and guardrails (timeouts, bounded retries, backoff, and circuit breaker behavior) to avoid cascading failures during KMS incidents.

B) Outage / degradation behavior (wording):
- “Redwood treats customer KMS as a critical dependency. When KMS latency or error rates exceed a threshold, Redwood degrades safely by limiting retry amplification, applying backpressure, and returning explicit errors to callers rather than increasing load on KMS. We provide dashboards and alerts on KMS dependency health.”

What NOT to disclose externally (keep high-level):
- Exact timeout values, retry counts, circuit breaker thresholds by environment.
- Any language implying we can always decrypt during KMS outage.

Answers to the two items you called out on the call:

1) KMS outage / degradation behavior
Redwood treats customer KMS as a critical dependency. When KMS latency or error rates degrade, Redwood applies operational guardrails (timeouts, bounded retries with backoff/jitter, and circuit breaker/backpressure behavior) to prevent retry amplification and request storms. If KMS is unavailable for an operation that requires key unwrapping, Redwood fails fast and returns explicit errors rather than continuing with unclear correctness.

2) “Do you cache DEKs? If KMS is down, can you still decrypt?”
Redwood uses envelope encryption. Data is encrypted with a DEK, and the DEK is wrapped by a customer-managed KEK in your KMS/HSM. For active workloads, Redwood may retain short-lived in-memory cryptographic material to serve requests efficiently (e.g., within a process lifetime), but we do not rely on indefinite caching of KMS responses. If your KMS is unavailable, Redwood may be unable to unwrap DEKs for decrypt operations that require a KMS call.

Sarah,

Answering inline at a high level; Naomi can schedule time with our team if you want to go deeper.

1) Dual-decrypt window
Yes. The rotation procedure supports a staged approach with a dual-decrypt (dual-read) window, so data encrypted under the prior key version can still be decrypted while the new key version is introduced and validated. The window is explicitly controlled to avoid partial/unknown states.

2) Rollback strategy
Rollback is supported during the cutover window. If decrypt failures or mismatch signals exceed thresholds, we can revert to the prior key version and re-validate. The procedure is designed to be reversible until completion criteria are met.

3) KMS outage behavior (reads/writes)
Redwood does not “guess” during a KMS outage. If a read/write path requires a KMS unwrap operation and KMS is unavailable, the system will return explicit errors for those operations. We also employ backpressure and bounded retries to avoid amplifying impact. Exact behavior depends on which component is calling KMS and the operation (encrypt vs decrypt), but the design principle is correctness + clear failure over silent degradation.

IAM scoping / separate principals
Yes. We support least-privilege IAM scoping for runtime operations (e.g., encrypt/decrypt) and recommend separating runtime principals from rotation/change principals.
